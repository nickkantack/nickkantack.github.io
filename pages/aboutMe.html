IntriCon had some winding machines that were built in the 70s and had controllers that were aging but which could no longer be replaced with newer versions. In my first week of work I accidentally corrupted the software of the last spare controller we had. I told my boss that I was 80% confident I could build a modern replacement for the controllers in the span of a week. Thus, I spent my entire second week of work poring over the internal workings of the winders, mapping their circuits, probing voltages during their operation, and designing a replacement controller built on an Atmega chip. At the end of the second week, I had successfully built a cheap and reliable replacement controller. I really enjoyed learning how to work with microcontrollers and the challenge of reverse engineering an old and complex machine. This was the first of several projects in automation and machine control that I got to build entirely from scratch.

IntriCon had a process for recording daily production numbers from each machine operator that consisted of the operator filling out a paper form with their totals for the day. Later, a shift manager would manually enter these totals into an excel spreadsheet. In this I saw an opportunity for automation that I could not pass up. I build a web application that ran locally on the company's internal network which allowed any operator to load a form and enter their production totals for the day (it was simply a digital version of the original paper form). The data were curated on the server and shift managers could trade their data entry time for using a sweet of analytic tools I built into the system. Managers could view trends in different metrics, compare data across shifts and operators. There was even an anomaly detection tool that would automatically send an email report that identified any dramatic changes in data that might need manager investigation. This was my first experience with building a data analytics web application and I enjoyed the challenge of creating intuitive ways for users to get the insights they're looking for in their data.

About Me

My career began at IntriCon, a medical device manufacturer in St Paul, MN. There I designed millimeter-scale electromagnetic coils for use in implantable medical devices.
