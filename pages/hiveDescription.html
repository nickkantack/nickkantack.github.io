The Challenge of Developing a Hive AI

Hive is a unique, dynamic, and complex game. Since discovering the game in 2020, I became very interested and strongly suspected that it was possible to make an AI that would outperform most humans. In searching for an existing, strong Hive AI, I discovered that a handful of Hive agent's have been built (e.g. a student paper, a graduate thesis, and an open source project), yet none seemed to achieve strong play strength (namely, the ability to consistently beat humans). I set out to try my hand at building a Hive agent that could, ideally, outperform any human opponent. I've already gotten my AI to the level of outperforming myself, but I'm actively developing it to try to achieve even stronger play.

Why is building a good Hive AI so challenging?

Hive has a relatively large branching factor (in the mid game, an average of about 70 according to experiments with my AI). One of the main reasons for so many legal moves is that a player can have a dozen hexes available to spawn new pieces and up to 8 types of pieces that could spawn in each (\(12 \times 8 =96\) legal spawns, with alternative moves available). By comparison, Go has at most 361 legal moves (at the opening, when black can play in any of the \(19 \times 19 = 361\) points). Imagine if Go had 8 different types of stones a player could play. More initial moves would be available even if the board were only 7 points long (\(7 \times 7 \times 8 = 392\)).

Compounding the branching factor is that determining which moves are legal requires much more computation for Hive than it does for other games. In Go, any open point is a legal move provided it satisfies Ko and is not a self-capture. For Hive, whether a piece can move involves first checking if the piece is structurally critical to the hive (moving it does not separate the hive), and that there is enough space for it to move. The former requires a consideration of the entire board, and the latter requires a recursive search of the local hive perimeter.

My Hive AI Design

The AI is built primarily as a NodeJS application. For the web UI linked above, the important components of the NodeJS project are ported over as javascript source files. The AI performs a breadth-first tree search (with alpha-beta pruning and a custom child-ordering algorithm to expedite the pruning) to a max depth, at which point undetermined game states receive estimated win probabilities using a small, 1 layer neural network. Currently, the AI can perform a complete search to a depth of 4 moves ahead in a reasonable amount of time (for the web UI linked above, an initial search to a depth of 2 is performed for the first move suggestions, and these suggestions are updated once a depth of 3 search finishes). I am currently in the process of refining the tree search and planning on offloading it to a GPU to allow evaluation at even greater move depths (with a goal of 6 moves deep).
