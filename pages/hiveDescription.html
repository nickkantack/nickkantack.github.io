The Challenge of Developing a Hive AI

Hive is a unique, dynamic, and complex game. Since discovering the game in 2020, I became very interested and strongly suspected that it was possible to make an AI that would outperform most humans. In searching for an existing, strong Hive AI, I discovered that a handful of Hive agent's have been built (e.g. a student paper, a graduate thesis, and an open source project), yet none seemed to achieve strong play strength (namely, the ability to consistently beat humans). I set out to try my hand at building a Hive agent that could, ideally, outperform any human opponent. I've already gotten my AI to the level of outperforming myself, but I'm actively developing it to try to achieve even stronger play.

Why is building a good Hive AI so challenging?

One major challenge with developing a Hive agent is calculating the list of possible moves. In a game like chess or Go, calculating a list of all currently legal moves is straightforward and computationally cheap. The mechanics of piece motion in Hive and the evolution of the game board over time causes the delineation of possible moves to be computationally more intensive. To be clear, developing algorithms that determine legal moves in Hive can be done methodically, but making these algorithms fast so as to allow deeper AI evaluation is much more difficult. For instance, the "one Hive" rule stipulates that the only pieces that can move are those which are not currently critical to keeping all played tiles as one connected group. In short, if removing a piece would cut the hive into to completely disjoint groups, then that piece cannot move. Unfortunately, whether a piece is mobile according to this rule can change on each move, even moves that concern very far away pieces. Thus far, a majority of my development effort has involved developing, refining, and accelerating these algorithms to minimize their computational footprint and allow deeper tree searches to discover stronger moves.
